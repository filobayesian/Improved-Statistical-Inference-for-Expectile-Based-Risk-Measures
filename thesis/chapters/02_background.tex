\chapter{Background}
\label{ch:background}

\section{Regular variation and the second-order condition}
\label{sec:bg:rv}

We consider a real-valued random loss $X$ that
has continuous distribution function $F$ and tail, or survival, function
$\bar F(x) \coloneqq 1 - F(x)$. Given an i.i.d. sample
$X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}$ from
$F$, the corresponding order statistics are denoted by
$X_{1:n} \le X_{2:n} \le \cdots \le X_{n:n}$.

Heavy-tailed asymptotics are governed by the language of regular
variation; the reference is \textcite[Appendix~B.1]{deHaanFerreira2006}.

\begin{definition}[Regular variation]
\label{def:bg:rv}
A Lebesgue measurable function $f : (0,\infty) \to \R$ that is eventually
positive is \emph{regularly varying at infinity with index} $\alpha \in \R$, written
$f \in \mathrm{RV}_{\alpha}$, if for every $y > 0$,
\[
  \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{f(t y)}{f(t)} \;=\; y^{\alpha}.
\]
The functions in $\mathrm{RV}_{0}$ are called \emph{slowly varying}.
\end{definition}

The first-order condition under which all of our asymptotics are taken
asserts that $F$ has a Pareto-type tail.

\begin{condition}[First-order condition]
\label{cond:bg:first-order}
There exists $\gamma > 0$ such that
$\bar F \in \mathrm{RV}_{-1/\gamma}$, or equivalently the
\emph{tail quantile function}
\[
  U(t) \;\coloneqq\; \inf\!\Big\{ x \in \R \,:\, \tfrac{1}{\bar F(x)} \ge t \Big\}
  \,,\qquad t>1,
\]
satisfies $U \in \mathrm{RV}_{\gamma}$.
\end{condition}

Throughout, for a continuous distribution function $F$, we write
\[
  \Quant_X(\tau)
  \;\coloneqq\;
  F^{\leftarrow}(\tau)
  =
  \inf\{x\in\R:F(x)\ge \tau\},
  \qquad 0<\tau<1.
\]
For continuous \(F\), this agrees with \(U(t)=F^\leftarrow(1-1/t)\). Thus
\[
  \Quant_X(\tau)=U((1-\tau)^{-1}),
  \qquad
  \Quant_X(1-p)=U(1/p).
\]

\Cref{cond:bg:first-order} is the Fr\'echet max-domain condition with
extreme-value index $\gamma$ \parencite[Theorem~1.2.1]{deHaanFerreira2006};
standard examples include Pareto, Burr, Student-$t$, Fr\'echet and
Fisher--Snedecor \(F\)-law tails \parencite[\S2.6]{deHaanFerreira2006}. For
normal limits we use the standard second-order refinement.

\begin{condition}[Second-order condition $\mathcal C_{2}(\gamma, \rho, A)$]
\label{cond:bg:second-order}
There exist $\gamma > 0$, a second-order parameter $\rho \le 0$, and a
measurable auxiliary function $A$ of constant sign with $A(t) \to 0$ as
$t \to \infty$, such that for every $y > 0$,
\[
  \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{A(t)}
    \!\left[ \frac{U(t y)}{U(t)} - y^{\gamma}\right]
  \;=\; y^{\gamma}\, \frac{y^{\rho} - 1}{\rho},
\]
where the right-hand side is read as $y^{\gamma} \log y$ when
$\rho = 0$.
\end{condition}

The function $|A|$ is itself regularly varying with index $\rho$
\parencite[Theorem~2.3.9]{deHaanFerreira2006}. Since $A$ has constant
sign eventually, signed ratios $A(t y)/A(t)$ converge to $y^\rho$; the
rate $A$ is the source of the second-order bias terms below.

\begin{condition}[Finite first-moment range]
\label{cond:bg:finite-first-moment}
In addition to \Cref{cond:bg:first-order}, the extreme-value index
satisfies $\gamma \in (0,1)$ and the negative part
$X^{-}=\max(-X,0)$ satisfies
\[
  \E X^{-}<\infty .
\]
\end{condition}

Under \Cref{cond:bg:first-order}, $\gamma<1$ gives $\E X^{+}<\infty$;
together with the displayed lower-tail condition this is the finite-mean
regime for the quantile-based expectile approximation.

\section{Tail-index and extreme-quantile estimation}
\label{sec:bg:tail-index-quantile}

We collect the two classical estimators used throughout this work: the
Hill estimator of $\gamma$ and the Weissman extrapolation for extreme
quantiles. The textbook reference is
\textcite[Ch.~3--4]{deHaanFerreira2006}.

\subsection{The Hill estimator}
\label{sec:bg:hill}

Given an i.i.d. sample $X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}$ from $F$ and an integer
$k = k_{n} \in \{1, \ldots, n-1\}$, the \emph{Hill estimator}
\parencite{Hill1975} of
$\gamma$ based on the $k + 1$ top order statistics is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:hill}
  \hat\gamma_{n}(k) \;=\; \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \log\!\left(
    \frac{X_{n-i+1\,:\,n}}{X_{n-k\,:\,n}} \right).
\end{equation}
Since $U(t)\to\infty$, $X_{n-k:n}>0$ with probability tending to one
for intermediate $k$; log-based estimators are understood on this event.
The consistency result
below is \textcite[Theorem~3.2.2]{deHaanFerreira2006}; the normal limit
is \textcite[Theorem~3.2.5]{deHaanFerreira2006} when $\rho<0$, with the
$\rho=0$ endpoint covered by the $m=1$ specialisation of
\textcite[Theorem~1]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:bg:hill}
Assume \Cref{cond:bg:first-order} with $\gamma > 0$. Let
$k = k_{n} \to \infty$ and $k / n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. Then
$\hat\gamma_{n}(k) \toprob \gamma$. If, in addition,
$\mathcal C_{2}(\gamma, \rho, A)$ holds and
$\sqrt{k}\, A(n/k) \to \lambda \in \R$, then
\[
  \sqrt{k}\,\big( \hat\gamma_{n}(k) - \gamma \big)
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N\!\left( \frac{\lambda}{1 - \rho},\; \gamma^{2} \right).
\]
\end{theorem}

The last citation is used only to justify the endpoint case
\(\rho=0\): with \(m=1\), the pooled-Hill central limit theorem reduces to the
same single-sample Hill statement under the displayed second-order centring.
The choice of $k$ controls the usual bias--variance trade-off; here it is
treated as an exogenous tuning sequence in the asymptotic analysis.

\subsection{Weissman extrapolation to extreme quantiles}
\label{sec:bg:weissman}

The \emph{Weissman extrapolation} \parencite{Weissman1978} extrapolates
from the intermediate-order statistic $X_{n - k:n}$ --- which
estimates the quantile $\Quant_{X}(1 - k/n)$ at the intermediate level
$1 - k/n$ --- to a quantile at a very-extreme level $1 - p$ with
$p = p_{n} \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. Plugging the Hill estimator into
the first-order identity $U(t y) / U(t) \to y^{\gamma}$ gives the
Weissman estimator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:weissman}
  \hatWei_{n}(1 - p \mid k)
  \;=\; \left( \frac{k}{n p} \right)^{\hat\gamma_{n}(k)}
        X_{n - k\,:\,n}.
\end{equation}
The theorem below, the Hill-estimator specialisation of
\textcite[Theorem~4.3.8]{deHaanFerreira2006}, covers the far-tail
regime $np=o(k)$; the case $np=O(1)$ is very-extreme in the sense that
only finitely many observations are expected beyond the target. Such rows are
included by the theorem whenever \(k/(np)\to\infty\) and
\(\sqrt{k}/\log\{k/(np)\}\to\infty\) both hold.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:bg:weissman}
Assume $\mathcal C_{2}(\gamma, \rho, A)$ with $\gamma > 0$ and
$\rho < 0$. Let $k = k_{n} \to \infty$, $k / n \to 0$, $p = p_{n} \to 0$
with $k / (np) \to \infty$ and $\sqrt{k} / \log(k / (np)) \to \infty$.
Suppose $\sqrt{k}\, A(n/k) \to \lambda \in \R$. Then
\[
  \frac{\sqrt{k}}{\log\!\big(k/(np)\big)}
  \left( \frac{\hatWei_{n}(1 - p \mid k)}{\Quant_{X}(1 - p)} - 1 \right)
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N\!\left( \frac{\lambda}{1 - \rho},\; \gamma^{2} \right).
\]
\end{theorem}

The log-denominator is the cost of extrapolation. Up to that factor the
limit is the Hill limit, which is why
\textcite[\S2.3]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} pool Weissman estimators
geometrically. This pooled-Weissman law is the source result against
which the pooled extreme-expectile estimator in
\Cref{ch:pooled-extreme} will be compared.

\section{Expectiles}
\label{sec:bg:expectiles}

Expectiles were introduced by \textcite{NeweyPowell1987} as
asymmetric-least-squares analogues of quantiles. Their role as coherent
and elicitable risk measures is developed in
\textcite{BelliniKlarMullerRosazzaGianin2014,Ziegel2016,Gneiting2011,EhmGneitingJordanKrueger2016}.

\subsection{Definition}

Let $X$ be a real-valued random variable with $\E |X| < \infty$, and
fix a level $\tau \in (0, 1)$. The $\tau$-\emph{expectile} of $X$ is
defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:expectile-def}
  \expectile{\tau}(X)
  \;\coloneqq\;
  \argmin_{\theta \in \R}\;
  \E\!\left[\, \eta_{\tau}(X - \theta) - \eta_{\tau}(X) \,\right],
  \qquad
  \eta_{\tau}(u) \coloneqq \abs{\tau - \one\{u \le 0\}}\, u^{2}.
\end{equation}
The subtraction of $\eta_\tau(X)$ makes the criterion finite under
$\E|X|<\infty$ and does not affect the minimiser; at $\tau=1/2$ one
recovers the mean.

The convex objective \eqref{eq:bg:expectile-def} has a unique
minimiser, and its first-order condition reduces to the implicit
equation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:expectile-foc}
  \tau\, \E\!\big[(X - \expectile{\tau}(X))_{+}\big]
  \;=\;
  (1 - \tau)\, \E\!\big[(\expectile{\tau}(X) - X)_{+}\big],
\end{equation}
where $u_{+} = \max(u, 0)$. Thus expectiles balance upper and lower
first partial moments, unlike quantiles, which balance tail
probabilities.

\subsection{Basic properties}

The following properties are immediate from
\eqref{eq:bg:expectile-def} or \eqref{eq:bg:expectile-foc}; we collect
them without proof and refer to \textcite[\S2]{NeweyPowell1987} and
\textcite[Sections~2--3]{BelliniKlarMullerRosazzaGianin2014} for
details.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:bg:expectile-properties}
Let $X$ and $Y$ be real-valued random variables with $\E |X|, \E |Y|
< \infty$. For each $\tau \in (0, 1)$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Existence and uniqueness.} $\expectile{\tau}(X)$ exists
      and is uniquely defined.
\item \textbf{Law invariance.} If $X$ and $Y$ have the same
      distribution, then $\expectile{\tau}(X)=\expectile{\tau}(Y)$.
\item \textbf{Mean recovery.} $\expectile{1/2}(X) = \E X$.
\item \textbf{Location equivariance.}
      $\expectile{\tau}(X + c) = \expectile{\tau}(X) + c$ for
      $c \in \R$.
\item \textbf{Positive homogeneity.}
      $\expectile{\tau}(\lambda X) = \lambda\, \expectile{\tau}(X)$
      for $\lambda \ge 0$.
\item \textbf{Sign reflection.}
      $\expectile{\tau}(-X) = -\expectile{1 - \tau}(X)$.
\item \textbf{Monotonicity in $\tau$.} The map
      $\tau \mapsto \expectile{\tau}(X)$ is nondecreasing on
      $(0, 1)$, and is strictly increasing when $X$ is nondegenerate,
      with the boundary limits
      $\expectile{\tau}(X) \to \mathrm{ess}\inf(X)$ as $\tau \to 0$
      and $\expectile{\tau}(X) \to \mathrm{ess}\sup(X)$ as
      $\tau \to 1$ (infinite limits allowed).
\item \textbf{Monotonicity in the argument.} If $X \le Y$ almost
      surely, then $\expectile{\tau}(X) \le \expectile{\tau}(Y)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

For losses, these properties give translation equivariance, positive
homogeneity and monotonicity. If $Y$ denotes a financial position and
$\rho_{\tau}(Y)=\expectile{\tau}(-Y)$ is the associated capital
requirement, then $\rho_\tau$ is coherent exactly when
$\tau\ge 1/2$ \parencite{BelliniKlarMullerRosazzaGianin2014}.
This is a statement about the capital requirement for positions under the
loss convention \( -Y \), not about subadditivity of the map
\(X\mapsto\expectile{\tau}(X)\) for arbitrary signed variables.
Expectiles are also elicitable on classes with finite first moment; the
asymmetric squared score is strictly consistent under finite second
moments, and the general scoring class has Bregman-type and
Choquet-type representations
\parencite{Gneiting2011,EhmGneitingJordanKrueger2016}. These facts
motivate high-level expectiles as tail-risk functionals, while the
asymptotic theory below uses only \eqref{eq:bg:expectile-foc} and the
extreme expectile--quantile equivalence.

\section{The high-expectile/high-quantile bridge}
\label{sec:bg:extreme-expectile}

\Cref{ch:pooled-extreme} uses expectile theory through the population
relationship between a high expectile and the quantile of the same
level. The stochastic extreme-quantile part is supplied by the pooled
Weissman estimator of \textcite[\S2.3]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021},
recalled in \Cref{sec:bg:pooling}; the expectile-specific work is to
control the deterministic bridge between $\expectile{\tau}$ and
$\Quant_X(\tau)$. This section therefore records only the bridge
statements needed later.

\subsection{The expectile--quantile asymptotic equivalence}

The single most important fact for extreme expectile inference is
that, in the heavy-tailed regime, the expectile is asymptotically
proportional to the quantile of the same level.

\begin{proposition}[Asymptotic equivalence;
                    \textcite{BelliniKlarMullerRosazzaGianin2014},
                    \textcite{DaouiaGirardStupfler2019}, Corollary~1 with $p=2$]
\label{prop:bg:expectile-quantile}
Suppose \Cref{cond:bg:finite-first-moment} holds. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:expectile-quantile-equivalence}
  \frac{\expectile{\tau}(X)}{\Quant_{X}(\tau)}
  \;\xrightarrow{\tau \uparrow 1}\;
  \psifn(\gamma) \;\coloneqq\; \big(\gamma^{-1} - 1\big)^{-\gamma}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

The bridge requires only the finite-mean range $\gamma<1$ and lower-tail
integrability; stronger restrictions used for direct empirical
expectile estimation are not part of the quantile-based bridge route.
The function $\psifn$ is continuous
on $(0,1)$, with
$\psifn(\gamma) \to 1$ as $\gamma \downarrow 0$ and
$\psifn(\gamma) \to \infty$ as $\gamma \uparrow 1$. It is \emph{not}
monotone: its logarithmic derivative
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:m-function}
  m(\gamma)
  \;\coloneqq\;
  \frac{d}{d\gamma}\log\psifn(\gamma)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{1 - \gamma} - \log\big(\gamma^{-1} - 1\big)
\end{equation}
changes sign at $\gamma^{\star}\approx0.2178$, where $\psifn$ reaches
its minimum. In the Chapter~3 pooled extreme-expectile statement, $m(\gamma)$
appears as the coefficient of the linear term in the Taylor expansion of the
bridge-Hill contribution. At the exceptional value where $m(\gamma)=0$, this
linear term vanishes and the expansion starts at quadratic order.

Under the second-order condition $\mathcal C_{2}(\gamma, \rho, A)$ with
$\gamma \in (0, 1)$ and $\E X^{-}<\infty$, the first-order equivalence
\eqref{eq:bg:expectile-quantile-equivalence} sharpens to the
second-order expansion
\parencite[Proposition~1(i)]{DaouiaGirardStupfler2020}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:c-gamma-rho}
  \begin{aligned}
  \frac{\expectile{\tau}(X)}{\Quant_{X}(\tau)}
  &=
  \psifn(\gamma)\Big[
      1 + c(\gamma, \rho)\,A\big((1 - \tau)^{-1}\big)
      + \gamma(\gamma^{-1} - 1)^{\gamma}
        \frac{\E X}{\Quant_{X}(\tau)}
  \\
  &\hspace{5.6em}
      + o\big(\lvert A((1 - \tau)^{-1})\rvert\big)
      + o\big(\Quant_{X}(\tau)^{-1}\big)
    \Big],
  \\
  c(\gamma, \rho)
  &\coloneqq
  \frac{(\gamma^{-1} - 1)^{-\rho}}{1 - \gamma - \rho}
  + \frac{(\gamma^{-1} - 1)^{-\rho} - 1}{\rho}.
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
At $\rho=0$ the second term in $c(\gamma,\rho)$ is read by continuity,
so $c(\gamma,0)=m(\gamma)$. The two visible deterministic pieces in
\eqref{eq:bg:c-gamma-rho} are the second-order tail-bias term
\[
  c(\gamma,\rho)A\big((1-\tau)^{-1}\big)
\]
and the first-moment bridge term
\[
  \gamma(\gamma^{-1}-1)^\gamma
  \frac{\E X}{\Quant_X(\tau)}.
\]
In \Cref{ch:pooled-extreme} the first is controlled through the
second-order tail rate, while the second is controlled on the
pooled-Weissman scale by the explicit bridge-rate condition.

\subsection{Relation to earlier expectile estimators}

The i.i.d. extreme-expectile literature also studies direct empirical
expectiles based on asymmetric least squares, LAWS-type estimators using
least asymmetrically weighted squares, quantile-based intermediate
plug-in estimators, and Weissman-type expectile extrapolations from an
intermediate expectile anchor
\parencite{DaouiaGirardStupfler2018,DaouiaGirardStupfler2020,DavisonPadoanStupfler2023}.
These results provide context for the use of the expectile--quantile
bridge, but they communicate or estimate a different object. The estimator
analysed in \Cref{ch:pooled-extreme} performs the extreme extrapolation in
the pooled-quantile component and then applies the population bridge at the
target level. Consequently, the later argument relies on the first- and
second-order bridge statements recorded in this section rather than on a
direct empirical-expectile central limit theorem.

\section{The optimal pooling framework}
\label{sec:bg:pooling}

We now recall the pooling framework of
\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}. For compact references in the simulation
chapter, this work sometimes abbreviates their pooling theory as DPS. The
framework provides the source
theory for pooled tail-index and extreme-quantile inference before the
expectile--quantile bridge is applied. The framework pools tail-index and
extreme-quantile estimators from $m$ samples, allowing heterogeneous
sizes, second-order properties and cross-sample tail dependence.
This general framework is recalled to identify the source objects and
notation. It is broader than the setting used later: the main Chapter~3
statement specialises back to independent samples with common margins.

\subsection{Setup}

Throughout \Cref{sec:bg:pooling}, $m$ is fixed while
$n=\sum_{j=1}^{m}n_j\to\infty$. Let
$\bm X_i=(X_{i,1},\ldots,X_{i,m})^\top$, $i\ge1$, be i.i.d. copies of
$\bm X=(X_1,\ldots,X_m)^\top$. Margin $j$ is observed for
$i=1,\ldots,n_j$ and has continuous right-heavy-tailed distribution
$F_j$, tail quantile function \(U_j(t)=F_j^\leftarrow(1-1/t)\), and
extreme-value index $\gamma_j>0$; its order statistics are denoted
$X_{1:n_j,j}\le\cdots\le X_{n_j:n_j,j}$.
We write $\Quant_j(\tau)=F_j^\leftarrow(\tau)$, so that
$U_j(t)=\Quant_j(1-1/t)$ for $t>1$.

Tail inference in sample $j$ uses its top $k_{j} + 1$ order statistics,
where the effective sample size $k_{j} = k_{j}(n)$ satisfies
$k_{j} \to \infty$ and $k_{j} / n_{j} \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. The
total effective sample size is $k = \sum_{j=1}^{m} k_{j}$. Assume the
asymptotic-proportionality conditions
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:pool-rates}
  \frac{n_{1}}{n_{j}} \;\to\; b_{j} \in (0, \infty),
  \qquad
  \frac{k_{1}}{k_{j}} \;\to\; c_{j} \in (0, \infty),
\end{equation}
with $b_{1} = c_{1} = 1$. The marginal $F_{j}$ satisfies
$\mathcal C_{2}(\gamma_{j}, \rho_{j}, A_{j})$
(\Cref{cond:bg:second-order}). The analysis is developed under the
\emph{common-tail-index} assumption
\[
  (H_{\gamma}) \qquad
  \gamma_{1} \;=\; \cdots \;=\; \gamma_{m} \;=\; \gamma.
\]
Under this assumption, all common-$\gamma$ vector limits below are
expressed on the aggregate scale $\sqrt{k}$, with the constants $c_j$
translating the local $\sqrt{k_j}$ rates.

The cross-sample asymptotic dependence is captured by a pairwise
\emph{tail-copula} structure
\parencite[\S2.1]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}.

\paragraph{Condition $\mathcal J(\bm R)$ (tail-copula condition).}
For each pair $(j, \ell)$ with $j \ne \ell$, there exists a function
$\Rjl : [0, \infty]^{2} \setminus \{(\infty, \infty)\} \to [0, \infty)$
such that, for every
$(x_{j}, x_{\ell}) \in [0, \infty]^{2} \setminus \{(\infty,\infty)\}$,
\[
  \lim_{s \to \infty}
    s\, \Prob\!\big(\,
      1 - F_{j}(X_{j}) \le x_{j}/s,\;
      1 - F_{\ell}(X_{\ell}) \le x_{\ell}/s\,\big)
  \;=\; \Rjl(x_{j}, x_{\ell}).
\]

The case $\Rjl \equiv 0$ is asymptotic tail independence, implied by the
independent samples used later in the document.

\subsection{The pooled Hill estimator}

Each sample contributes a Hill estimator $\hat\gamma_{j}(k_{j})$ as
in \eqref{eq:bg:hill}; we collect these into the random vector
\[
  \hat{\bm\gamma}_{n}
  \;=\;
  \big(\hat\gamma_{1}(k_{1}), \ldots, \hat\gamma_{m}(k_{m})\big)^{\top}.
\]
Given a weight vector $\bm\omega \in \R^{m}$ with $\bm\omega^{\top}
\bm 1 = 1$, the \emph{pooled Hill estimator} is the weighted arithmetic
combination
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:pooled-hill}
  \hat\gamma_{n}(\bm\omega)
  \;\coloneqq\;
  \bm\omega^{\top}\hat{\bm\gamma}_{n}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{j=1}^{m} \omega_{j}\,\hat\gamma_{j}(k_{j}).
\end{equation}
The affine constraint $\bm\omega^\top\bm 1=1$ ensures consistency for the
common $\gamma$ under $(H_\gamma)$.

The fundamental asymptotic result is the joint CLT for the marginal
Hill estimators.

\begin{theorem}[\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}, Theorem~1]
\label{thm:bg:pool-hill}
Assume $\mathcal C_{2}(\gamma_{j}, \rho_{j}, A_{j})$ for every $j$,
the tail-copula condition $\mathcal J(\bm R)$, the
proportionality conditions \eqref{eq:bg:pool-rates}, and
$\sqrt{k_{j}}\, A_{j}(n_{j}/k_{j}) \to \lambda_{j} \in \R$ for every
$j$. Then
\[
  \big(\sqrt{k_{1}}(\hat\gamma_{1}(k_{1}) - \gamma_{1}),
       \ldots,
       \sqrt{k_{m}}(\hat\gamma_{m}(k_{m}) - \gamma_{m})\big)^{\top}
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N(\bm B, \bm V),
\]
with $\bm B = \big(\lambda_{j}/(1 - \rho_{j})\big)_{j=1}^{m}$ and
$\bm V$ the symmetric matrix whose entries are
\[
  V_{j, \ell}
  \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \gamma_{j}^{2}, & j = \ell, \\[2pt]
    \displaystyle
    \frac{\gamma_{j}\gamma_{\ell}}
         {\max(b_j,b_\ell)\sqrt{c_jc_\ell}}\,
      \Rjl(b_jc_\ell,\, b_\ell c_j),
        & j < \ell.
  \end{cases}
\]
The matrix is symmetric, so $V_{\ell,j}=V_{j,\ell}$ for $\ell>j$.
Suppose, in addition, the common-$\gamma$ condition
$(H_{\gamma})$. Then the same marginal estimators, now viewed on the
common $\sqrt{k}$ scale, satisfy
\[
  \sqrt{k}\,\big(\hat{\bm\gamma}_{n}-\gamma\bm 1\big)
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N(\Bc,\Vc),
\]
where
\[
  (\Bc)_j
  =
  \sqrt{c_j\sum_{i=1}^{m} c_i^{-1}}\,
  \frac{\lambda_j}{1-\rho_j}
  \qquad j=1,\ldots,m.
\]
The covariance matrix $\Vc$ is given by
\[
  (\Vc)_{j,\ell}
  =
  \left(\sum_{i = 1}^{m} c_i^{-1}\right)
  \begin{cases}
    \gamma^{2}c_j, & j=\ell, \\[2pt]
    \displaystyle
    \frac{\gamma^{2}}{\max(b_j,b_\ell)}\,
      \Rjl(b_jc_\ell,b_\ell c_j),
      & j<\ell,
  \end{cases}
\]
with $(\Vc)_{\ell,j}=(\Vc)_{j,\ell}$ for $\ell>j$.
Consequently, for any $\bm\omega$ with $\bm\omega^{\top}\bm 1 = 1$,
\[
  \sqrt{k}\,\big(\hat\gamma_{n}(\bm\omega) - \gamma\big)
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N\!\big(\bm\omega^{\top}\Bc,\;
                    \bm\omega^{\top}\Vc\bm\omega\big).
\]
\end{theorem}

Here \(b_j\) records the limiting relative sample size and \(c_j\) records the
limiting relative threshold size. The vector \(\Bc\) collects the local
second-order Hill biases after conversion to the aggregate \(\sqrt{k}\) scale,
while \(\Vc\) is the corresponding covariance matrix. In the independent
distributed case used in Chapter~3, \(\Vc\) becomes diagonal.
For independent samples, all off-diagonal entries vanish and $\Vc$
becomes the diagonal matrix
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:V_c-diagonal}
  \Vc
  \;=\;
  \gamma^{2}\!\left(\sum_{i = 1}^{m} c_{i}^{-1}\right)
    \mathrm{diag}(c_{1}, \ldots, c_{m}).
\end{equation}
\Cref{eq:bg:V_c-diagonal} is the covariance form to be used when the
main Chapter~3 statement is specialised to independent samples.

\subsection{Variance- and AMSE-optimal weights}

The relevant weights solve quadratic programmes under the affine
constraint $\bm\omega^\top\bm 1=1$; unless stated otherwise, they need
not be nonnegative. In \Cref{ch:pooled-extreme}, these formulas are
applied only to the pooled-Weissman weights; they do not define or
optimise the separate bridge-Hill weights introduced there.

\begin{proposition}[Optimal weights;
                    \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}, Theorem~1;
                    estimated-weight implementation in \S2.2 and Corollary~1]
\label{prop:bg:optimal-weights}
Suppose $\Vc$ is positive definite. Under the common-$\gamma$
condition $(H_{\gamma})$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item The variance-optimal weights, minimising
      $\bm\omega^{\top}\Vc\bm\omega$ subject to
      $\bm\omega^{\top}\bm 1 = 1$, are
      \[
        \bm\omega^{\mathrm{Var}}
        \;=\;
        \frac{\Vc^{-1}\bm 1}{\bm 1^{\top}\Vc^{-1}\bm 1},
      \]
      with corresponding asymptotic variance
      $\bm\omega^{\mathrm{Var},\top}\Vc\bm\omega^{\mathrm{Var}}
        = (\bm 1^{\top}\Vc^{-1}\bm 1)^{-1}$.
\item The AMSE-optimal weights, minimising
      $(\bm\omega^{\top}\Bc)^{2} + \bm\omega^{\top}\Vc\bm\omega$
      subject to $\bm\omega^{\top}\bm 1 = 1$, are
      \[
        \bm\omega^{\mathrm{AMSE}}
        \;=\;
        \frac{(1 + \Bc^{\top}\Vc^{-1}\Bc)\,\Vc^{-1}\bm 1
              - (\bm 1^{\top}\Vc^{-1}\Bc)\,\Vc^{-1}\Bc}
             {(1 + \Bc^{\top}\Vc^{-1}\Bc)(\bm 1^{\top}\Vc^{-1}\bm 1)
              - (\bm 1^{\top}\Vc^{-1}\Bc)^{2}}.
      \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

In the independent-samples case, $\Vc$ is diagonal
(\Cref{eq:bg:V_c-diagonal}) and the variance-optimal weights reduce
to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:V-opt-distrib}
  \omega_{j}^{\mathrm{Var}}
  \;=\;
  \Bigg(\sum_{i = 1}^{m} c_{i}^{-1}\Bigg)^{\!\!-1}\!
  c_{j}^{-1}
  \;\sim\;
  \frac{k_{j}}{k},
\end{equation}
where the equivalence uses $c_{i}^{-1} = \lim k_{i}/k_{1}$. The
finite-sample distributed version is
\[
  \tilde{\bm\omega}_{n}^{\mathrm{Var}}
  =
  (k_{1}/k,\ldots,k_{m}/k)^{\top},
\]
which is exactly normalised and converges to $\bm\omega^{\mathrm{Var}}$.

Feasible weights replace $\Vc$ and $\Bc$ by consistent plug-ins
\parencite[\S2.2]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}. If
$\hat{\bm\omega}^{\top}\bm 1=1$ exactly and
$\hat{\bm\omega}\toprob\bm\omega$, then
$\hat\gamma_n(\hat{\bm\omega})$ is $\sqrt{k}$-equivalent to
$\hat\gamma_n(\bm\omega)$; the difficult practical step is stable
estimation of $\Vc$ and the bias quantities.

\subsection{Weighted geometric pooling of extreme quantiles}

For estimation of an extreme quantile $q(1 - p)$ with $p = p(n) \to
0$, each sample contributes a Weissman estimator
\[
  \hat q_{j}^{\star}(1 - p \mid k_{j})
  \;=\;
  \left(\frac{k_{j}}{n_{j}\, p}\right)^{\!\hat\gamma_{j}(k_{j})}\!
  X_{n_{j} - k_{j} : n_{j},\,j}.
\]
Because Weissman extrapolation is multiplicative, pooling is geometric
on the original scale, equivalently linear on the log scale. It requires
asymptotic equivalence of the marginal extreme quantiles:
\begin{condition}[Tail homoskedasticity $(\mathcal H)$]
\label{cond:bg:tail-homoskedasticity}
For every $j \ne \ell$, $U_{j}(t) / U_{\ell}(t) \to 1$ as $t \to
\infty$.
\end{condition}
Equivalently, $\Quant_j(1-p)/\Quant_\ell(1-p)\to1$ as $p\downarrow0$.
The pooled extreme-quantile estimator of
\textcite[\S2.3]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:pooled-weissman}
  \hatWei_{n}(1 - p \mid \bm\omega)
  \;\coloneqq\;
  \prod_{j = 1}^{m}
    \big[\hat q_{j}^{\star}(1 - p \mid k_{j})\big]^{\omega_{j}}.
\end{equation}
Taking logs reduces pooling to weighted sums of the local identities
$\log \hat q_{j}^{\star}(1 - p \mid k_{j})
  = \log(k_{j}/(n_{j} p))\,\hat\gamma_{j}(k_{j})
    + \log X_{n_{j} - k_{j} : n_{j},\,j}$;
so the Hill part is handled by \Cref{thm:bg:pool-hill};
\eqref{eq:bg:pool-rates} gives
$\log(k_j/(n_jp))=\log(k/(np))+O(1)$.

\begin{theorem}[\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}, Theorem~2]
\label{thm:bg:pool-weissman}
Assume the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:bg:pool-hill}, the common-$\gamma$
condition $(H_{\gamma})$, the tail-homoskedasticity
condition $(\mathcal H)$, and, in addition, $\rho_j<0$ for every
$j=1,\ldots,m$. Let $p = p(n) \to 0$ with
$k/(np) \to \infty$ and $\sqrt{k}/\log(k/(np)) \to \infty$. Then for
any $\bm\omega$ with $\bm\omega^{\top}\bm 1 = 1$ and any
$j \in \{1, \ldots, m\}$,
\[
  \frac{\sqrt{k}}{\log\!\big(k/(np)\big)}
  \!\left(\frac{\hatWei_{n}(1 - p \mid \bm\omega)}{\Quant_{j}(1 - p)}
            - 1\right)
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N\!\big(\bm\omega^{\top}\Bc,\;
                    \bm\omega^{\top}\Vc\bm\omega\big).
\]
\end{theorem}

The tail-homoskedasticity condition and the restrictions \(\rho_j<0\) are
essential for transferring the extreme-quantile result to a common target. They
ensure that recentering by any marginal extreme quantile is asymptotically
harmless on the Weissman scale.
The strict $\rho_j<0$ assumption is part of the Weissman theorem.
Together with the bias-rate assumptions
$\sqrt{k_j}\,A_j(n_j/k_j)\to\lambda_j$ in
\Cref{thm:bg:pool-hill}, it also supplies the rate needed to recenter
by any marginal $\Quant_j(1-p)$ under $(\mathcal H)$
\parencite[Supplementary Material, Lemma~B.2]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}: for any
$j,\ell$,
\[
  \log\Quant_j(1-p)-\log\Quant_\ell(1-p)
  =
  O\!\big(\abs{A_j(1/p)}\big)
  +
  O\!\big(\abs{A_\ell(1/p)}\big)
  =
  o(k^{-1/2}),
\]
along the Weissman sequence.

Thus the pooled Weissman and pooled Hill limits share the same mean,
variance and optimal weights up to the log-rate factor. This shared
structure is the reason \Cref{ch:pooled-extreme} can use the weight
criteria of \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} for the pooled-Weissman
component.

\subsection{Estimated weights and plug-in objects}
\label{sec:bg:estimated-pooling}

The optimal weights above are oracle quantities because they depend on
the asymptotic bias and covariance objects. For inference they are
replaced either by deterministic allocation weights, such as
\((k_1/k,\ldots,k_m/k)^\top\) in the independent distributed case, or
by estimated weights based on plug-in estimates of the same objects.
The following result records the stability property used in
\Cref{ch:pooled-extreme}. Estimated weights are used there only in the
pooled-Weissman role; the bridge-Hill vector remains deterministic.

\begin{proposition}[Estimated pooling weights;
                    \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021},
                    Supplementary Material, Theorem~A.1; Theorem~2 and Corollary~8]
\label{prop:bg:estimated-pooling-weights}
Assume the common-\(\gamma\) conditions of \Cref{thm:bg:pool-hill}. Let
\(\widehat{\bm\omega}_n\) be a random weight vector satisfying
\[
  \widehat{\bm\omega}_n^\top\bm 1=1,
  \qquad
  \widehat{\bm\omega}_n\toprob\bm\omega,
\]
for some deterministic \(\bm\omega\) with
\(\bm\omega^\top\bm 1=1\). Then
\[
  \sqrt{k}\,\big(\hat\gamma_n(\widehat{\bm\omega}_n)-\gamma\big)
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N(\bm\omega^\top\Bc,\bm\omega^\top\Vc\bm\omega).
\]
If, in addition, the assumptions of \Cref{thm:bg:pool-weissman} hold,
then
\[
  \frac{\sqrt{k}}{\log(k/(np))}
  \left(
    \frac{\hatWei_n(1-p\mid\widehat{\bm\omega}_n)}
         {\Quant_j(1-p)}
    -1
  \right)
  \;\todist\;
  \mathcal N(\bm\omega^\top\Bc,\bm\omega^\top\Vc\bm\omega),
  \qquad j=1,\ldots,m.
\]
In the independent common-distribution distributed case, the same
statement holds with the diagonal covariance matrix
\eqref{eq:bg:V_c-diagonal} and the distributed bias constants of
\textcite[Corollary~5]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}.
\end{proposition}

The Supplementary Material's Theorem~A.1 is the generic estimated-weight transfer:
exact affine normalisation and convergence in probability of the weights
make the random-weight pooled estimator asymptotically equivalent to the
pooled estimator with limiting deterministic weights. Applied to
\Cref{thm:bg:pool-hill}, this gives the Hill statement. The Weissman
statement is the corresponding random-weight version of
\Cref{thm:bg:pool-weissman}; in the distributed common-marginal case it
is the published Corollary~8 of
\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}.

The next proposition separates the probabilistic consistency of the
estimated bias and variance objects from the algebra of applying a
chosen weight vector.

\begin{proposition}[Plug-in bias and variance]
\label{prop:bg:plugin-bias-variance}
Let \(\widehat{\Bc}_n\) and \(\widehat{\Vc}_n\) be estimators of
\(\Bc\) and \(\Vc\) satisfying
\[
  \widehat{\Bc}_n\toprob\Bc,
  \qquad
  \widehat{\Vc}_n\toprob\Vc,
\]
and let \(\widehat{\bm\omega}_n\toprob\bm\omega\). Define
\[
  \widehat B_{\widehat{\bm\omega},n}
  =
  \widehat{\bm\omega}_n^\top\widehat{\Bc}_n,
  \qquad
  \widehat V_{\widehat{\bm\omega},n}
  =
  \widehat{\bm\omega}_n^\top
  \widehat{\Vc}_n
  \widehat{\bm\omega}_n.
\]
Then
\[
  \widehat B_{\widehat{\bm\omega},n}
  \toprob
  \bm\omega^\top\Bc,
  \qquad
  \widehat V_{\widehat{\bm\omega},n}
  \toprob
  \bm\omega^\top\Vc\bm\omega.
\]
\end{proposition}

All vectors and matrices have fixed dimension. The maps
\((w,b)\mapsto w^\top b\) and
\((w,V)\mapsto w^\top Vw\) are continuous, so the result follows from
the continuous mapping theorem.

\textcite[\S2.2 and Corollary~1]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} give one way to
obtain the consistency assumptions in \Cref{prop:bg:plugin-bias-variance}
through estimated second-order and tail-dependence quantities. That construction
is not assumption-free. In the source it is stated under:
\begin{itemize}
\item \(\rho_j<0\) for all margins;
\item the parametric second-order form \(A_j(t)=\gamma\beta_j t^{\rho_j}\);
\item positive definiteness of the asymptotic covariance matrix;
\item consistency of the \(\beta_j\) estimators;
\item \((\hat\rho_j-\rho_j)\log n_j=o_P(1)\).
\end{itemize}
In the common-distribution distributed case, their Corollaries~6--7 give the
corresponding deterministic variance allocation and plug-in AMSE constructions
under the analogous common second-order parameter consistency assumptions.

The log-scale interval for the expectile estimator in
\Cref{ch:pooled-extreme} is derived from the transferred plug-in
centring and variance objects after the expectile decomposition.

\subsection{Distributed inference as a special case}
\label{sec:bg:pool-distrib}

\noindent\textbf{Case used in Chapter~3.}
In the independent common-distribution distributed setting, the samples
are i.i.d. subdivisions of one common population. Then:
\begin{itemize}
\item the common-$\gamma$ condition $(H_{\gamma})$ and the
      tail-homoskedasticity condition $(\mathcal H)$ are trivially
      satisfied;
\item the tail copula $\Rjl$ between any pair of distinct samples
      vanishes identically (independence implies tail independence);
\item $\Vc$ in \Cref{thm:bg:pool-hill} becomes the diagonal matrix
      \eqref{eq:bg:V_c-diagonal};
\item the variance-optimal weights of \Cref{prop:bg:optimal-weights}
      reduce to $\omega_{j}^{\mathrm{Var}} \propto k_{j}$ as in
      \eqref{eq:bg:V-opt-distrib}.
\end{itemize}

Under the stronger balanced-allocation assumptions studied by
\textcite[Corollary~6, Theorem~3 and Corollary~8]{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021},
the variance-optimally pooled Hill and pooled Weissman estimators match
the corresponding unfeasible estimators built from the entire combined
sample at first order. This oracle equivalence is useful benchmark
context for distributed inference. The main Chapter~3 statement uses the more
direct consequence needed for the
expectile problem: in the independent common-distribution case, the
pooled-Weissman bias and variance objects are the diagonal distributed
objects displayed above.
