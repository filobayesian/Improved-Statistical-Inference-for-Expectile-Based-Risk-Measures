\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

Estimating tail risk is challenging because the events of greatest practical
interest are precisely those for which the data are scarce. In financial,
insurance and operational-risk applications, the target may lie beyond the
range where empirical averages or order statistics alone give reliable
answers. Extreme-value methods address this by combining intermediate tail
information with a heavy-tailed model, trading a small amount of structural
assumption for the ability to extrapolate to rarer loss levels. This is the
regime studied here, with a focus on extreme expectiles as tail-risk
functionals.

Expectiles, introduced by \textcite{NeweyPowell1987}, are asymmetric
least-squares analogues of quantiles. At high levels they respond to both the
size and probability of large losses, and they have attractive risk-measure
properties, including coherence in the relevant high-level range and
elicitability under standard integrability conditions
\parencite{BelliniKlarMullerRosazzaGianin2014,Ziegel2016,Gneiting2011}.
For heavy-tailed distributions with finite mean, extreme expectiles are also
asymptotically proportional to extreme quantiles. In the notation used
throughout this work,
\[
  \expectile{\tau}
  \sim
  \psifn(\gamma)Q(\tau),
  \qquad
  \psifn(\gamma)=(\gamma^{-1}-1)^{-\gamma},
  \qquad \tau\uparrow1,
\]
where \(Q\) is the marginal quantile function and \(\gamma\) is the
extreme-value index. This bridge is the point at which expectile inference can
be connected to the more developed theory of extreme-quantile extrapolation.

The second ingredient is pooling. In many statistical settings the available
sample is naturally distributed across several machines, institutions or data
sources. If these sources share the same upper-tail target, each local sample
contains useful information but none is necessarily ideal on its own.
\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} develop an optimal weighted-pooling
framework for tail-index and extreme-quantile inference, including geometric
pooling of local Weissman estimators. Their results identify variance- and
AMSE-optimal weights for the pooled extreme-quantile problem and provide
estimated-weight and plug-in inference machinery under source conditions. The
question addressed here is how much of that structure survives after the target
is changed from an extreme quantile to an extreme expectile.

The estimator studied here applies the expectile--quantile bridge after the
pooled extreme-quantile extrapolation. For a target level
\(\tau_n=1-p_n\), define
\[
  \widehat\xi_{\tau_n}^{\mathrm{pool},\star}(\bm\nu,\bm\omega)
  =
  \psifn\!\big(\widehat\gamma_n(\bm\nu)\big)\,
  \widehat q_n^\star(\tau_n\mid\bm\omega).
\]
The vector \(\bm\omega\) weights the geometrically pooled Weissman estimator
\(\widehat q_n^\star(\tau_n\mid\bm\omega)\). The vector \(\bm\nu\) weights
only the pooled Hill estimator appearing in the outer bridge factor. This
separation is important. The diagonal choice \(\bm\nu=\bm\omega\) is a
natural prototype, but it is not forced by the construction and is not treated
as an optimality principle.

With this estimator in place, this work makes three contributions. First, it
formulates and supports a first-order log-scale limit for the pooled
extreme-expectile estimator from an exact decomposition of its error. In the
finite-\(m\) iid common-marginal setting, the resulting statement identifies
the leading term as the pooled-Weissman error. The
outer bridge-Hill term and the population expectile--quantile bridge error are
lower order under the bridge-rate condition used in the main Chapter~3
statement. Thus the first-order bias, variance and AMSE criteria concern
\(\bm\omega\), while \(\bm\nu\) remains first-order unidentified.

Second, this work formulates a corresponding transfer of the relevant
estimated-weight and plug-in inference machinery from the optimal-pooling
framework of \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} to the expectile estimator
when the source consistency conditions apply. Estimated weights are used only
in the pooled-Weissman role. For implementation, the bridge-Hill vector is kept
at the deterministic threshold-allocation convention
\(\bm\nu_n^k=(k_1/k,\ldots,k_m/k)^\top\).

Third, this work reports a finite-sample study matched to this theoretical
scope. The simulations vary the pooled-Weissman weights, keep the bridge-Hill
convention fixed, and assess point estimation, plug-in intervals and
decomposition diagnostics. Their role is to show how the Chapter~3
first-order route behaves in the studied designs, not to introduce a wider
estimator class or a new optimality principle.

The rest of this work is organised as follows. \Cref{ch:background} recalls
the heavy-tail, expectile and optimal-pooling results used as source inputs.
\Cref{ch:pooled-extreme} develops the pooled extreme-expectile estimator, its
exact decomposition, the deterministic first-order statement, the weight
consequences and the estimated-weight inference transfer statement.
\Cref{ch:finite-sample} reports the finite-sample study for the proposed
estimator and inference route. \Cref{ch:conclusion} summarises the main
lessons, discusses the
limitations of the proposed estimator and inference route, and outlines
directions for further work.
