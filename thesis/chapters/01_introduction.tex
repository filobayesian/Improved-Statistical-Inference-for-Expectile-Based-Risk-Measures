\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:intro:motivation}

Quantitative risk management routinely needs to summarise the tail of a
loss distribution by a single number. The industry-standard summary has
long been the Value-at-Risk (VaR), defined as a high quantile of the loss
distribution. VaR is intuitive and easy to compute, but it suffers from
two well-known shortcomings that have shaped the search for alternatives.
First, VaR is not subadditive: it can deem a diversified portfolio more
risky than its components, contradicting the diversification principle and
disqualifying VaR from the class of \emph{coherent} risk measures in the
sense of \textcite{ArtznerDelbaenEberHeath1999}. Expected Shortfall (ES)
corrects this defect and has progressively replaced VaR in regulatory
practice. However, ES is not \emph{elicitable}~\parencite{Gneiting2011}:
no scoring function has its expectation uniquely minimised at the true ES,
so ES forecasts cannot be ranked or backtested directly.

Expectiles are the only law-invariant risk measure that is simultaneously
coherent (for confidence levels $\tau \ge 1/2$) and elicitable
\parencite{BelliniKlarMullerRosazzaGianin2014}. Originally introduced by
\textcite{NeweyPowell1987}, the $\tau$-expectile of a random loss $X$ is
defined as the minimiser of an asymmetric squared loss:
\[
  \expectile{\tau}(X) \;=\; \argmin_{\theta \in \R}\;
    \E\!\left[\,\abs{\tau - \one\{X \le \theta\}}\,(X-\theta)^2 \,\right].
\]
Setting $\tau = 1/2$ recovers the mean; setting $\tau \to 1$ moves the
expectile into the right tail. Because the asymmetric loss is squared
rather than absolute, expectiles --- unlike quantiles --- are sensitive
to the \emph{magnitude} of tail losses, not just to their frequency.
\textcite{EhmGneitingJordanKrueger2016} place expectiles within a broader
theory of consistent scoring functions and Choquet representations.

This thesis addresses inference for expectiles at levels $\tau$ close to
one, in two situations that are increasingly relevant in practice:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item the loss distribution is heavy-tailed, in the sense of having an
      extreme-value index $\gamma > 0$, so that the empirical
      asymmetric-least-squares estimator becomes unstable as $\tau$ approaches
      one and extreme-value theory becomes the natural framework;
\item the data are spread across several independent samples --- whether
      because of distributed computation, regulatory data-sharing
      restrictions, or natural heterogeneity between studies --- and
      inference must be conducted by communicating only summary statistics
      between sources.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Extreme expectile inference}
\label{sec:intro:extreme-expectiles}

The central bridge between expectile estimation and classical
extreme-value theory is the \emph{expectile--quantile asymptotic
equivalence}. Under heavy-tailed assumptions with extreme-value index
$\gamma \in (0, 1/2)$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:intro:asymptotic-equivalence}
  \frac{\expectile{\tau}(X)}{\Quant_X(\tau)}
  \;\xrightarrow{\tau \uparrow 1}\;
  \psifn(\gamma) \;\coloneqq\; \big(\gamma^{-1} - 1\big)^{-\gamma},
\end{equation}
where $\Quant_X(\tau)$ is the $\tau$-quantile of $X$. Two families of
estimators arise naturally from \eqref{eq:intro:asymptotic-equivalence}.
The \emph{asymmetric-least-squares} (LAWS) estimator minimises the
empirical asymmetric criterion over the data; the \emph{quantile-based}
(QB) estimator substitutes consistent estimators of $\gamma$ and of an
extreme quantile of $X$ into the asymptotic-equivalence formula. Their
asymptotic theory at intermediate levels and via Weissman-type
extrapolation to very-extreme levels was developed for iid heavy-tailed
data by \textcite{DaouiaGirardStupfler2018} and subsequent work, and
extended to the dependent setting by \textcite{DavisonPadoanStupfler2023};
to the multivariate setting by \textcite{PadoanStupfler2022}. The relevant
machinery is reviewed in \Cref{ch:background}.

\section{Pooled inference across samples}
\label{sec:intro:pooling}

When $m$ samples are available, with heterogeneous sizes and possibly
heterogeneous tails, the natural question is how best to combine them.
\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} develop an optimal pooling framework
for the tail index $\gamma$ and for extreme quantiles. Their estimators
take the form of weighted arithmetic combinations (for the tail index)
and weighted \emph{geometric} combinations (for extreme quantiles), the
latter specifically adapted to the multiplicative-power structure of the
Weissman extrapolation. They derive the joint asymptotic normality of
the per-sample estimators across $m$ samples, allowing for asymptotic
dependence between samples driven by a tail-copula, and identify the
variance-optimal and AMSE-optimal pooling weights in closed form. In the
important special case of \emph{distributed inference} --- where data are
iid both within and across machines --- the cross-sample tail-copula
vanishes, off-diagonal entries of the joint asymptotic covariance
collapse to zero, and the pooled estimators recover the asymptotic
efficiency of the unfeasible Hill / Weissman estimator built from the
combined sample.

The framework of \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} is the methodological
foundation of this thesis. The framework does not address expectiles; the
question of how to combine $m$ pairs $(\hat\gamma_j, \hat\Quant_j(p_j))$
into a coherent inference procedure for an extreme expectile
$\expectile{\tau_n}$ has not been studied.

\section{Research question and scope}
\label{sec:intro:question}

The thesis answers the following question:
\begin{quote}
\itshape Given $m$ independent samples of iid heavy-tailed observations,
with each sample $j$ contributing point estimates $(\hat\gamma_j,
\hat\Quant_j(p_j))$ together with their joint asymptotic covariance, how
should one combine these summary statistics to perform inference and
Weissman-style extrapolation for an extreme expectile of the common
underlying distribution?
\end{quote}
The scope is restricted as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item the data within each sample are univariate iid (no time-series
      dependence), matching the independent common-distribution
      distributed setting of
      \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021};
\item the loss distribution is heavy-tailed with extreme-value index
      $\gamma \in (0, 1/2)$ --- the standard range that guarantees the
      existence of the expectile and the validity of the
      asymptotic equivalence \eqref{eq:intro:asymptotic-equivalence};
\item the input to the pooling procedure is summary statistics, not raw
      data, in line with practical distributed-inference constraints.
\end{itemize}

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro:contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item We define a \emph{pooled extreme-expectile estimator} at
      intermediate levels by feeding the pooled tail-index estimator
      $\hat\gamma_n(\bm\omega_\gamma)$ and the pooled weighted-geometric
      Weissman extreme-quantile estimator $\hatWei_n(\,\cdot\,;\bm\omega_q)$
      of \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} into the
      asymptotic-equivalence formula
      \eqref{eq:intro:asymptotic-equivalence}.
\item We derive the joint asymptotic distribution of the pooled inputs,
      accounting for the cross-source structure (which is diagonal
      because the samples are independent) and for the within-source
      dependence between $\hat\gamma_j$ and $\hat\Quant_j$ induced by the
      use of overlapping top order statistics.
\item Applying the delta method through $\psifn$ to this joint pooled
      CLT, we obtain the asymptotic normality of the pooled
      intermediate-level expectile estimator together with explicit
      forms of its asymptotic bias and variance.
\item We characterise the \emph{variance-optimal} and \emph{AMSE-optimal}
      pooling weights tailored to the pooled expectile estimator. These
      need not coincide with the weights optimal for the tail index or
      for the extreme quantile alone, owing to the non-trivial dependence
      between the two pieces of information.
\item We extend the construction to very-extreme levels $\tau_n^{\prime}
      \uparrow 1$ with $n(1-\tau_n^{\prime}) \to c < \infty$ via a
      Weissman-style extrapolation of the pooled intermediate-level
      estimator, and derive the corresponding central limit theorem
      by feeding the joint intermediate-level limit of the previous
      contribution into the plug-in CLT of
      \textcite[Proposition~C.6]{DavisonPadoanStupfler2023}.
      As in the single-sample case, the extrapolated estimator inherits
      the asymptotic variance of the pooled tail-index estimator.
\item We construct asymptotic confidence intervals for extreme expectiles
      and a test for \emph{expectile-tail-homogeneity} across the $m$
      samples, modelled on the tail-quantile-homogeneity test of
      \textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021} and on the GLS-weighted
      equality-of-means test of \textcite[Section~3.3]{PadoanStupfler2022}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Outline of the thesis}
\label{sec:intro:outline}

\Cref{ch:background} collects the technical background: regular
variation and the second-order condition, the Hill and Weissman
estimators, expectiles and their characterisation, single-sample iid
extreme-expectile inference, and the optimal pooling framework of
\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}.
\Cref{ch:pool-int} develops the pooled estimator at intermediate
expectile levels and the associated asymptotic theory.
\Cref{ch:pool-ext} treats the Weissman-style extrapolation to
very-extreme expectile levels.
\Cref{ch:discussion} discusses limitations, the link to Expected
Shortfall, and avenues for future work, including extensions to
time-series dependence and multivariate observations.
Detailed proofs are gathered in \Cref{app:proofs}.
