\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

\section{Motivation and question}
\label{sec:intro:motivation}

Tail risk is usually reported through a single high-level functional of
the loss distribution. Value-at-Risk is transparent but not coherent in
the sense of \textcite{ArtznerDelbaenEberHeath1999}; Expected Shortfall
is coherent but not elicitable on its own \parencite{Gneiting2011}.
Expectiles occupy the useful intersection: for $\tau\ge1/2$ they are
coherent and elicitable
\parencite{BelliniKlarMullerRosazzaGianin2014}, and because they are
defined through asymmetric squared loss they react to the magnitude of
tail losses, not only to their frequency.

At extreme levels, expectile estimation becomes an extreme-value
problem. In the heavy-tailed finite-mean regime, the
expectile--quantile equivalence
$\expectile{\tau}/\Quant_X(\tau)\to\psifn(\gamma)
=(\gamma^{-1}-1)^{-\gamma}$ as $\tau\uparrow1$ makes a
quantile-based (QB) route natural: estimate the tail index, estimate an
extreme quantile, and plug both into the bridge. The single-sample
theory is developed by
\textcite{DaouiaGirardStupfler2018,DaouiaGirardStupfler2020}; the
plug-in extrapolation lemma used here comes from
\textcite{DavisonPadoanStupfler2023}.

This thesis asks how that QB expectile route should be pooled when the
data are distributed. Starting from the optimal-pooling framework of
\textcite{DaouiaPadoanStupfler2021}, each source communicates exactly a
local Hill estimator, a local Weissman extreme-quantile estimator, and
their asymptotic covariance. The research question is how to combine
these Paper-3 primitives to estimate, extrapolate and make inference on
an extreme expectile of a common heavy-tailed distribution.

\section{Scope}
\label{sec:intro:question}

The setting is deliberately narrow. The samples are univariate, iid
within source, independent across sources, and share one common marginal
distribution. The QB theory is used under $\gamma\in(0,1)$ and
$\E X^-<\infty$; the stronger $\gamma<1/2$ finite-variance range enters
only for direct LAWS comparisons. No raw observations, local LAWS
expectiles, or expectile-specific summaries are communicated. The
intermediate order statistic is already contained in the Weissman
primitive and is not an additional shared input. Time-series dependence,
multivariate samples, real-data applications and alternative
communication protocols are left as future work.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro:contributions}

The contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item a pooled QB expectile estimator at intermediate levels, built only
      from pooled Hill and weighted-geometric Weissman primitives, with
      $\sqrt{k}$-equivalence between pooling primitives first and pooling
      matched local QB plug-ins first;
\item a joint pooled Gaussian limit for the tail-index and log-quantile
      inputs, followed by the delta-method CLT for the pooled
      intermediate expectile, including explicit bias and variance;
\item variance- and AMSE-optimal expectile weights. Under equal
      effective fractions they collapse to the Paper-3
      effective-sample-size weights; outside that regime the expectile
      target has its own formulas;
\item feasible log-scale confidence intervals and an
      expectile-tail-homogeneity diagnostic, with a noncentral
      chi-square limit in general and a central limit under the
      undersmoothing and equal-fraction regimes used for inference;
\item a very-extreme Weissman extrapolation, obtained by feeding the
      Chapter~3 input CLT into
      \textcite[Supplement, Proposition~C.6]{DavisonPadoanStupfler2023};
      at this scale the first-order variance and AMSE are governed by
      the pooled Hill component;
\item a reproducible simulation study comparing naive,
      variance-weighted, plug-in AMSE, oracle AMSE and full-sample
      benchmarks for intermediate and very-extreme expectile targets.
\end{enumerate}
