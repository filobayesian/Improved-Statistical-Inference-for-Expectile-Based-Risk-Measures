\chapter{Finite-sample study}
\label{ch:finite-sample}

This chapter reports a finite-sample study for the estimator developed in
\Cref{ch:pooled-extreme}. The purpose is deliberately narrow. We study the
pooled extreme-expectile estimator
\[
  \widehat\xi_{\tau}^{\mathrm{pool},\star}
    (\bm\nu_n^k,\bm\omega)
  =
  \psifn\!\big(\hat\gamma_n(\bm\nu_n^k)\big)
  \hatWei_n(1-p\mid\bm\omega),
  \qquad
  \bm\nu_n^k=(k_1/k,\ldots,k_m/k)^\top,
\]
where \(p=1-\tau\) and \(k=\sum_{j=1}^{m}k_j\). Thus the bridge-Hill
weights are fixed at the deterministic threshold-allocation convention,
whereas the pooled-Weissman weights \(\bm\omega\) vary across the
Daouia--Padoan--Stupfler choices studied below. The simulations do not claim
optimality of \(\bm\nu_n^k\), do not use random bridge-Hill weights, and do
not revive the intermediate expectile-pooling route discarded earlier.

\section{Estimators and implementation}
\label{sec:sim:estimators}

For each machine \(j\), the local Hill estimator and Weissman quantile
estimator are computed using the \texttt{HTailIndex} and \texttt{extQuantile}
routines from the \texttt{ExtremeRisks} package \parencite{ExtremeRisksR}.
The simulation code only adds the thesis-specific pooling layer: geometric
pooling of the local Weissman estimates, the map \(\psifn\), the deterministic
bridge-Hill convention \(\bm\nu_n^k\), and the summary calculations.

The following estimators are recorded in every scenario:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{equal}: \(\omega_j=1/m\);
\item \emph{DPS variance}: \(\omega_j=k_j/k\), the finite-sample independent
      distributed version of the DPS variance weights;
\item \emph{DPS AMSE oracle}: the DPS AMSE formula evaluated with the
      population second-order parameters of the data-generating model;
\item \emph{DPS AMSE plug-in}: the same formula with second-order inputs
      estimated using \texttt{evt0} \parencite{evt0R};
\item \emph{centralised}: the corresponding single-sample estimator computed
      from the combined sample, used only as a benchmark.
\end{itemize}
All distributed estimators use the same bridge-Hill vector
\(\bm\nu_n^k\). In particular, the plug-in AMSE estimator randomises
\(\bm\omega\) only.

For each estimator, inference is assessed with the log-scale interval from
\Cref{cor:pool-ext:plugin-inference}. In a Monte Carlo replication, if
\(\widehat B_\omega\) and \(\widehat V_\omega\) denote the selected DPS
centring and variance objects, the interval is
\[
  \widehat\xi
  \left[
    \exp\!\left\{-\frac{\ell}{\sqrt{k}}
      \big(\widehat B_\omega+z_{0.975}\sqrt{\widehat V_\omega}\big)\right\},
    \exp\!\left\{-\frac{\ell}{\sqrt{k}}
      \big(\widehat B_\omega-z_{0.975}\sqrt{\widehat V_\omega}\big)\right\}
  \right],
  \qquad
  \ell=\log\!\left(\frac{k}{np}\right).
\]
The interval is included as a diagnostic of the first-order plug-in route,
not as a claim of finite-sample calibration.

\section{Design}
\label{sec:sim:design}

The study stays within the iid common-marginal setting of the theorem. The
data-generating models are Pareto with \(\gamma=0.25\), Pareto with
\(\gamma=0.60\), Burr with \(\gamma=0.25\) and \(\rho=-1\), Burr with
\(\gamma=0.60\) and \(\rho=-1\), and the absolute Student distribution with
three degrees of freedom. The latter has \(\gamma=1/3\). The true expectile
\(\xi_\tau\) is computed numerically from the population expectile equation
\[
  \tau\,\E[(X-\xi_\tau)_+]
  =
  (1-\tau)\,\E[(\xi_\tau-X)_+],
\]
rather than by the asymptotic expectile--quantile bridge.

The final simulation uses \(5000\) Monte Carlo replications per scenario. The
main design has \(n=10000\), \(k_j/n_j=0.05\), \(np=5\),
\(m\in\{5,10\}\), and either balanced allocation or a strong imbalanced
allocation with machine proportions increasing geometrically from \(1\) to
\(10\). Two sensitivity blocks then fix \(m=10\) and the strong allocation:
one varies \(k_j/n_j\in\{0.03,0.05,0.10\}\), and the other varies
\(np\in\{1,5,10\}\).

Performance is measured by the Monte Carlo mean of the log relative error,
the root mean squared log relative error, empirical coverage of the nominal
\(95\%\) log interval, average log interval width, invalid-estimate rate, and
weight-stability diagnostics.

\section{Point estimation}
\label{sec:sim:point}

\Cref{tab:sim:point-main} reports the representative main design with
\(m=10\), strong sample-size imbalance, \(k_j/n_j=0.05\), and \(np=5\). The
distributed DPS variance estimator tracks the centralised benchmark closely
across all five models. The log RMSE is essentially unchanged in the Burr
heavy case, the Student case, and the Pareto heavy case, and differs only
slightly in the lighter-tailed Pareto and Burr settings.

The plug-in AMSE weights do not deliver a systematic improvement over the
variance weights in this design. This is not surprising: in the common
distributed setting the variance allocation is already the dominant stable
choice, and the estimated second-order quantities add finite-sample noise.
The conclusion is therefore practical rather than theoretical. The
simulation supports the use of DPS variance weights as a stable baseline for
\(\bm\omega\); it does not suggest opening a new optimisation layer for
\(\bm\nu\).

\section{Plug-in interval performance}
\label{sec:sim:intervals}

\Cref{tab:sim:interval-main} shows that the first-order log intervals are
not uniformly well calibrated at this sample size. Coverage is close to
nominal only in the absolute Student case, where the DPS variance and plug-in
AMSE intervals cover about \(92\%\) of replications. In the Pareto and Burr
cases, coverage is materially below \(95\%\), especially for the lighter-tailed
models. The centralised benchmark displays the same qualitative undercoverage,
so this is not mainly a distributed-pooling artefact. It is a finite-sample
calibration issue for the first-order log approximation and the plug-in
centring objects.

The interval results therefore support a cautious interpretation of
\Cref{cor:pool-ext:plugin-inference}. The corollary gives the asymptotic
studentisation when the DPS source route supplies consistent plug-in objects;
the finite-sample study shows that the resulting interval can still be
noticeably under-calibrated in moderate samples and biased tail models.

\Cref{fig:sim:bridge-diagnostic} gives a targeted diagnostic for this
undercoverage. It evaluates the same oracle first-order interval against both
the exact population expectile and the first-order bridge target
\(\psifn(\gamma)Q(1-p)\). In the light Pareto and Burr designs, much of the
coverage loss against the exact expectile disappears when the bridge target is
used, and exact-target coverage improves in the larger-\(n\), smaller
\(k/n\) diagnostic design. This supports the interpretation that the main
undercoverage is a plausible finite-sample bridge and first-order calibration
effect, not a sign error or a failure of the pooling construction. The
diagnostic is not uniform across all heavy-tailed designs, so the conclusion
remains cautionary rather than corrective.

\section{Sensitivity to thresholds and targets}
\label{sec:sim:sensitivity}

The threshold sensitivity in \Cref{tab:sim:threshold} shows the usual
bias--variance trade-off. For the Burr light model, increasing
\(k_j/n_j\) reduces log RMSE but worsens coverage sharply, consistent with
second-order bias becoming visible. For the absolute Student model, the
larger threshold \(0.10\) increases log RMSE. The Pareto heavy case benefits
from a larger threshold in RMSE terms, reflecting the absence of second-order
bias in the exact Pareto model. No single threshold is uniformly best across
models and metrics.

\section{Weight stability and limitations}
\label{sec:sim:limitations}

\Cref{tab:sim:weights} reports the AMSE weight diagnostics in the
representative imbalanced design. The oracle and plug-in AMSE weights are
stable in this grid: negative weights are essentially absent, and the average
maximum absolute weight is close to the largest deterministic allocation
weight. The stability of the weights is one reason the point-estimation
performance of DPS variance and plug-in AMSE weights is nearly identical in
\Cref{tab:sim:point-main}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\small
\caption{AMSE weight diagnostics in the representative main design.}
\label{tab:sim:weights}
\resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\input{tables/simulation/weight_stability}}
\end{table}

The simulation evidence is intentionally limited. It supports the promoted
estimator in the iid common-marginal distributed setting and suggests that
DPS variance weights are a robust practical choice for \(\bm\omega\). It also
shows that first-order plug-in intervals may under-cover at realistic sample
sizes. The study does not address non-identical margins, time series,
multivariate targets, random bridge-Hill weights, or lower-order optimality
of \(\bm\nu\). Those questions would require additional mathematical work
before they could become simulation targets.
